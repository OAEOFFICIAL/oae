Cloze Tests: Mastering the Art of Missing Words

Real-World Example: Imagine you're listening to your favourite song by Burna Boy, and suddenly, your phone network cuts out for a few seconds. When the song comes back on, a word or two is missing from a line. But because you know the song so well, and you understand the story it's telling, your brain automatically fills in the missing words! For example, if you hear, "Last last, na everybody go ____," even if "chop breakfast" was cut out, your mind quickly supplies it because you know the common phrase and the context of the song. That's exactly how a Cloze Test works – you use the words around the gap, your understanding of grammar, and your vocabulary to fill in the missing piece! It's like being a detective for words!
What is a Cloze Test?

My dear students, a Cloze Test is a very common type of English language exercise you will find in exams like WAEC, JAMB, NECO, and even scholarship tests. Simply put, it's a passage of text where some words have been removed, and replaced with blanks or spaces. Your job, as the brilliant student you are, is to fill in those blanks with the most appropriate words from a given list of options (multiple choice) or sometimes, to come up with the words yourself without any options.

The main goal of a Cloze Test is to check your understanding of English. It's not just about knowing individual words, but about how well you can put them together to make sense. It tests your vocabulary (do you know enough words?), your grammar (do you know how words fit together correctly?), your comprehension (do you understand the overall message of the passage?), and even your ability to identify idiomatic expressions or common collocations (words that usually go together). Think of it as a comprehensive workout for your English language skills!

Detailed Explanation: Strategies for Conquering Cloze Tests

Success in Cloze Tests isn't just about luck; it's about having a strategy, my smart students! These tests are designed to assess your holistic understanding of language, and with the right approach, you can ace them. Let's break down the essential steps and tips you need to master.

1. Read the Entire Passage First (Very Important!)

Before you even think about filling a single blank, take a moment to read the entire passage from beginning to end, skipping over the blanks. Don't worry about trying to guess the answers yet. The goal here is to get a general understanding of the topic, the main idea, the tone (is it serious, funny, informative?), and the overall flow of the text. This first read gives you the big picture, which is crucial context for making informed decisions later. Imagine trying to fix a car engine without knowing what the car is even used for – it would be difficult, right? Same with Cloze Tests!

2. Focus on the Sentences Around Each Blank

Once you have the general idea, go back and tackle each blank one by one. For each blank, pay very close attention to the sentence it is in, and also the sentences immediately before and after it. These surrounding words and sentences provide what we call "context clues." These clues are like breadcrumbs leading you to the right answer. They can tell you about the tense of the verb needed, the type of noun, or the relationship between ideas. For example, if a sentence says, "The boy felt very ____ after losing his football match," the word "losing" gives you a strong clue that the missing word describes a negative emotion.

3. Identify the Part of Speech Needed

This is a major key to success! For each blank, ask yourself: What kind of word is missing here? Is it a noun (a person, place, or thing), a verb (an action word), an adjective (describes a noun), an adverb (describes a verb, adjective, or another adverb), a preposition (shows relationship, like 'in', 'on', 'at'), or a conjunction (joins words or sentences, like 'and', 'but', 'or')?

  • Nouns: Often come after articles (a, an, the) or adjectives.
  • Verbs: Show action or state of being, and change based on the subject and tense.
  • Adjectives: Describe nouns and usually come before them or after linking verbs (is, was, seems).
  • Adverbs: Modify verbs, adjectives, or other adverbs, often ending in -ly.
  • Prepositions: Link nouns/pronouns to other words in a sentence, showing location, time, or direction.
  • Conjunctions: Connect words, phrases, or clauses.
Knowing the part of speech will immediately help you eliminate many wrong options, especially in multiple-choice questions.

4. Check for Grammatical Agreement and Tense

English grammar rules are your friends here!

  • Subject-Verb Agreement: If the subject is singular, the verb must be singular (e.g., "He runs"). If the subject is plural, the verb must be plural (e.g., "They run").
  • Tense Consistency: Look at the verbs around the blank. Is the passage written in the past tense, present tense, or future tense? The missing verb or auxiliary verb must match the overall tense of the passage or sentence. If the story is about something that happened yesterday, don't pick a present tense verb!
  • Pronoun Agreement: If a pronoun is missing, ensure it agrees in number and gender with the noun it refers to.
These grammatical checks can narrow down your choices significantly.

5. Consider Vocabulary, Collocations, and Idioms

Sometimes, multiple words might seem grammatically correct, but only one makes perfect sense in the context of the passage. This is where your vocabulary and knowledge of common English expressions come into play.

  • Vocabulary: Choose the word that has the most precise and appropriate meaning for the context.
  • Collocations: These are words that naturally go together (e.g., "make a mistake," not "do a mistake"; "heavy rain," not "strong rain").
  • Idioms: These are phrases whose meaning isn't obvious from the individual words (e.g., "kick the bucket" means to die). If an idiom is being used, you need to know the specific missing word that completes it.
Practising reading widely will help you build your vocabulary and familiarity with collocations.

6. Eliminate Wrong Options (If Multiple Choice)

If you're given options, use the process of elimination. As you apply the strategies above (part of speech, grammar, context), you'll find that some options are clearly incorrect. Cross them out! This makes it easier to choose from the remaining, more plausible options. Don't just pick the first word that sounds good; evaluate all options carefully.

7. Reread the Passage with Your Chosen Words

After you've filled in all the blanks, read the entire passage again with your chosen words inserted. Does it flow smoothly? Does it make complete sense? Is it grammatically sound? This final check helps you catch any mistakes and ensures that the passage reads naturally and coherently. If something sounds awkward or wrong, chances are you need to reconsider one or more of your choices.

Worked Examples & Applications

Example 1 (Multiple Choice)

The early bird ____ the fattest worm. (A) catch (B) caught (C) catches (D) catching

Step 1: Read the sentence and identify the subject.

The subject is "The early bird," which is singular.

Why? To determine the correct verb form for subject-verb agreement.
Step 2: Determine the tense and part of speech needed.

This is a common proverb, usually stated in the present simple tense. We need a verb.

Why? Proverbs express general truths and typically use the present simple.
Step 3: Evaluate the options based on agreement and tense.

(A) "catch" - Incorrect for singular subject in present tense (would be "catch" for plural like "birds catch").
(B) "caught" - Past tense, doesn't fit the general truth of a proverb.
(C) "catches" - Correct for singular subject ("bird") in present simple tense.
(D) "catching" - A present participle; would need an auxiliary verb (e.g., "is catching") to form a complete verb phrase.

Why? Eliminating options that violate grammatical rules or contextual meaning.

Answer: (C) catches

Example 2 (No Options - Fill in the blank)

Despite the heavy rain, the children continued their game ____ playing football in the field.

Step 1: Read the full sentence and understand the relationship between the clauses.

The first part talks about heavy rain (a negative condition), and the second part talks about the children continuing to play. There's a contrast: despite the rain, they kept playing.

Why? To understand the logical connection the missing word needs to establish.
Step 2: Identify the part of speech and meaning needed.

We need a word that shows the manner or way they continued their game, specifically 'playing football'. A preposition or an adverb indicating continuance or manner is likely.

Why? This helps narrow down possible word types and functions.
Step 3: Brainstorm words that fit the context and structure.

Common phrases related to continuing an activity are "by," "with," "through," or simply "they kept playing football." In this sentence, "continued their game ____ playing football," the most natural connection is showing the means or specific activity of the game. "By" or "of" could work, but "by" is more direct for means. "Of" would imply possession. "Playing football" is an action that defines "their game." So, "by" or possibly "through" could fit.

Why? Exploring suitable words that maintain coherence and grammatical correctness.
Step 4: Choose the most appropriate word and reread.

If we use "by," it reads: "The children continued their game by playing football..." This sounds natural and correct. "Through" could also work, but "by" is a more common and direct way to express the means of action here.

Why? Final verification for natural flow and meaning.

Answer: by

Watch Out! (Exam Trap)

A common mistake students make in Cloze Tests, especially in multiple-choice exams like JAMB and WAEC, is picking the first option that "sounds good" without fully considering the grammar, context, or other options. For example, if the options are "affect," "effect," "affects," "effects," and you need a verb in the present tense for a singular subject, choosing "effect" (which is mostly a noun) just because it's related to the meaning, would be wrong. You must pay attention to the part of speech and subject-verb agreement. Always check if the word you pick fits BOTH grammatically and semantically (in terms of meaning) into the sentence and the overall passage. Don't rush; take your time to analyse each blank thoroughly!

Conclusion & Summary

My brilliant students, Cloze Tests are not meant to confuse you, but to strengthen your command of the English language. By consistently applying the strategies we've discussed – reading for context, identifying parts of speech, checking grammar, and choosing the most appropriate vocabulary – you will not only improve your scores but also become a more proficient English speaker and writer. Remember, practice makes perfect! The more you read and practice, the better you will become at spotting those missing words and filling them in with confidence. Keep shining brightly!

  • Read the whole passage first: Get the general meaning and context.
  • Analyze around the blank: Use clues from words before and after.
  • Identify part of speech: Determine if a noun, verb, adjective, etc., is needed.
  • Check grammar: Ensure subject-verb agreement, correct tense, and pronoun agreement.
  • Consider meaning and common usage: Choose the most fitting word, paying attention to collocations and idioms.
  • Eliminate wrong options: Rule out choices that are grammatically incorrect or don't make sense.
  • Reread and verify: Ensure the completed passage flows well and is coherent.
