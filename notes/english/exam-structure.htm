What is the JAMB Use of English Exam Structure?

The JAMB Use of English exam structure refers to the specific way the questions are organised, the different sections covered, the number of questions in each section, and the type of skills JAMB is testing. It’s not just a random collection of English questions; it’s a carefully designed test to evaluate your proficiency in various aspects of the English language. Think of it as a house with different rooms: a living room, a bedroom, a kitchen – each with its own purpose and what you’d find there. Similarly, the JAMB Use of English exam has distinct sections, and knowing what kind of questions you'll meet in each "room" is half the battle won.

Understanding this structure is like having a map to navigate a new city. Without it, you might wander aimlessly, but with the map, you can plan your route, identify landmarks, and reach your destination faster and more efficiently. For your JAMB Use of English exam, this means knowing precisely where to focus your study efforts, how to allocate your time during the exam, and what kind of challenges to anticipate. This knowledge boosts your confidence, reduces exam anxiety, and significantly improves your chances of scoring high and securing admission to your dream university.

Detailed Explanation

The JAMB Use of English examination typically comprises 60 questions and is usually divided into three main sections. These sections are designed to assess different aspects of your English language skills: your reading comprehension, your knowledge of grammar and vocabulary (often called Lexis and Structure), and your understanding of spoken English (Oral Forms). Let's break down each of these crucial sections so you know exactly what to prepare for.

1. Comprehension/Reading Passages (Approx. 20-25 Questions)

This section is all about how well you can read, understand, and interpret written text. JAMB will provide you with several reading passages – usually between three to five passages of varying lengths and topics. After each passage, you will find a set of questions that you need to answer based *only* on the information provided in the text. This is a critical skill, as many students tend to use their outside knowledge, which can sometimes lead to incorrect answers if it contradicts the passage. The topics of these passages can be anything from science and history to social issues, current affairs, or even literature.

The questions in this section will test various abilities, such as identifying the main idea or topic of the passage, understanding the meaning of specific words or phrases as used in the context of the passage, drawing inferences (making logical deductions based on the information given), identifying the author's purpose or tone, and even recognising literary devices if the passage is fictional. This section is often the first part of the exam, and it requires careful reading, concentration, and the ability to process information quickly and accurately. Don't rush through the passages; take your time to read them at least twice – first for a general understanding, and then a second time to pinpoint answers to specific questions.

2. Lexis and Structure (Approx. 25-30 Questions)

The Lexis and Structure section is arguably the broadest and most challenging for many students because it covers a wide range of grammar, vocabulary, and sentence construction rules. "Lexis" refers to words and vocabulary, while "Structure" refers to how words are put together to form correct sentences and expressions. This section is designed to test your mastery of the rules that govern the English language. It’s where your knowledge of synonyms, antonyms, idioms, tenses, concord, and correct sentence patterns will be put to the test.

Within this section, you will typically encounter several sub-types of questions:

  • Synonyms: Choosing the word or phrase nearest in meaning to a given word.
  • Antonyms: Choosing the word or phrase opposite in meaning to a given word.
  • Sentence Completion/Fill in the Gaps: Selecting the most appropriate word or phrase to complete a sentence, often testing vocabulary, prepositions, phrasal verbs, or idiomatic expressions.
  • Idiomatic Expressions: Understanding the non-literal meaning of common idioms.
  • Concord/Agreement: Ensuring that subjects and verbs agree in number (e.g., "The boy plays" vs. "The boys play").
  • Tenses: Correct usage of past, present, and future tenses, as well as perfect and continuous forms.
  • Parts of Speech: Identifying and correctly using nouns, verbs, adjectives, adverbs, pronouns, conjunctions, prepositions, and interjections.
  • Sentence Construction/Rearrangement: Identifying grammatically correct sentences or arranging jumbled parts of a sentence into a coherent whole.
  • Punctuation: While less frequent, questions on correct punctuation might appear.
This section requires consistent practice with a wide range of vocabulary and a thorough understanding of grammatical rules. It's not enough to simply "sound right"; you need to know the underlying rules.

3. Oral Forms (Approx. 10-15 Questions)

The Oral Forms section focuses on your understanding of spoken English, specifically phonetics and phonology. Many students often neglect this section, thinking it's less important, but it carries significant marks and can be the difference between getting into your desired course or not. This part of the exam tests your ability to differentiate between sounds, understand stress patterns, and recognise correct pronunciation.

Questions in Oral Forms usually fall into these categories:

  • Vowel and Consonant Sounds: Identifying words that have the same vowel sound or consonant sound as a given word or underlined part of a word. For example, which word has the same vowel sound as 'cat' (e.g., 'mat', 'far', 'mate').
  • Stress Patterns: Identifying the syllable that is stressed in a multi-syllabic word. For instance, knowing that in 'photoGRAPH', the stress is on the second syllable, while in 'PHOto', it's on the first.
  • Intonation: Though less common in multiple-choice, understanding the rise and fall of speech can sometimes be tested implicitly.
To excel in this section, you need to be familiar with phonetic symbols and practice identifying sounds and stress patterns. Listening to spoken English (news, documentaries, educational programs) and actively paying attention to how words are pronounced can be very helpful. This section truly tests your ear for English and your knowledge of pronunciation rules.

In total, the JAMB Use of English exam usually consists of 60 multiple-choice questions. It is a compulsory subject for all JAMB candidates, regardless of their chosen course of study. The time allocated for the entire JAMB examination (including your other three subjects) is typically 2 or 3 hours (depending on the year and total questions), so you need to learn to manage your time wisely, allocating roughly 45 minutes to 1 hour for the Use of English section. Every question carries equal weight, so mastering all three sections is crucial for a high score.

Worked Examples & Applications

Example 1 (Comprehension)

Read the passage and answer the question:
"Deforestation, the clearing of forests by logging or burning, significantly contributes to climate change. Trees absorb carbon dioxide, a greenhouse gas, from the atmosphere. When forests are destroyed, this carbon dioxide is released back into the atmosphere, intensifying the greenhouse effect. This process also leads to loss of biodiversity and soil erosion."
Question: According to the passage, what is one major environmental consequence of deforestation?

Step 1: Read the Passage Carefully

Read through the passage once to get the general idea, then read it again, paying attention to details, especially keywords like "contributes to," "leads to," "results in."

Why? To fully grasp the context and identify direct answers or clues for inference.
Step 2: Locate Keywords in the Question

The question asks for a "major environmental consequence of deforestation." Look for phrases in the passage that describe effects or results of deforestation.

Why? To guide you to the specific part of the passage that contains the answer.
Step 3: Identify the Answer from the Passage

The passage states: "Deforestation... significantly contributes to climate change," and "This process also leads to loss of biodiversity and soil erosion." All these are major environmental consequences. So, any of these (climate change, loss of biodiversity, soil erosion) would be a correct answer, depending on the options provided.

Why? The answer must be directly supported by the text, not your prior knowledge.
Example 2 (Lexis and Structure - Antonym)

Choose the word that is opposite in meaning to the underlined word:
The speaker delivered an incredibly profound speech.

Options: A. simple B. complex C. deep D. thoughtful

Step 1: Understand the Underlined Word

What does 'profound' mean? It means something very deep, significant, insightful, or wise. In this context, a "profound speech" is one that is full of deep meaning and wisdom.

Why? You cannot find the opposite if you don't know the original meaning.
Step 2: Evaluate the Options for Opposite Meaning

A. simple: Not complicated, easy to understand. This seems like a good opposite for 'deep' or 'complex'.

B. complex: Intricate, difficult. This is closer to a synonym for profound, not an antonym.

C. deep: A direct synonym for profound.

D. thoughtful: Showing care, considered. This is related but not an opposite.

Why? You systematically compare each option to the target word's meaning to find the best fit.
Step 3: Select the Best Opposite

The most fitting opposite for 'profound' (deep, complex, significant) is 'simple'.

Answer: A. simple

Why? It directly contrasts the core meaning of 'profound'.
Example 3 (Oral Forms - Stress)

Choose the option where the stress is on the first syllable:

Options: A. re-CORD (verb) B. CON-duct (noun) C. be-GIN D. un-DER-stand

Step 1: Pronounce Each Word Aloud (mentally or softly)

Say each word and listen carefully to which part of the word you emphasize or say with more force.
A. re-CORD (re-CORD – stress on the second syllable)

B. CON-duct (CON-duct – stress on the first syllable, as a noun, e.g., 'good conduct')

C. be-GIN (be-GIN – stress on the second syllable)

D. un-DER-stand (un-DER-stand – stress on the third syllable)

Why? Oral forms are about sounds. Saying the words helps you identify the stress pattern.
Step 2: Identify the Word with Stress on the First Syllable

From the pronunciation, 'CON-duct' (as a noun) has the stress on the first syllable.

Answer: B. CON-duct

Why? This directly answers the question's requirement.
Watch Out! (Exam Trap)

A very common mistake students make in JAMB Use of English is neglecting the Oral Forms section. Many assume it's too difficult or less important than comprehension and lexis. This is a huge trap! The Oral Forms questions carry equal marks, and they are often straightforward if you've done even a little bit of practice. Ignoring this section means you are willingly giving up easy marks, which can drastically affect your overall score and your chances of admission. Don't be that student! Dedicate time to understanding vowel/consonant sounds and stress patterns. Another trap is failing to read comprehension passages carefully or answering based on what you *think* you know, rather than what the passage *states*. Always refer back to the text!

Also, watch out for "similar-sounding" words in the Lexis and Structure section. JAMB often uses options that look or sound alike but have completely different meanings or grammatical functions. For instance, confusing 'affect' (verb) with 'effect' (noun/verb) or 'complement' with 'compliment'. Always confirm the meaning and grammatical role of the word in context before choosing your answer. Proper time management is also crucial; don't spend too much time on one difficult question at the expense of other easier ones. If a question is proving too tough, make an educated guess (if you have eliminated some options) and move on, then come back if you have time.

By understanding the three main sections – Comprehension, Lexis and Structure, and Oral Forms – and the types of questions found within each, you can prepare more effectively, manage your exam time wisely, and confidently tackle any question that comes your way. Remember, consistent practice across all these areas is the key to unlocking an excellent score. So, go forth and conquer!
