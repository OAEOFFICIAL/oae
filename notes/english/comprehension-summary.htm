Comprehension & Summary: Unlocking Meaning and Condensing Ideas

Real-World Example: Imagine your mum sends you to the market with a very long list of items: "Garri, rice, beans, palm oil, groundnut oil, Maggi cubes, salt, fresh pepper, tomatoes, onions, frozen fish, and some sachet milk." After you get back home and put everything away, your dad asks you, "My pikin, what did your mum ask you to buy at the market today?" You wouldn't recite the entire long list word-for-word, would you? Instead, you'd likely say something like, "She asked me to buy food items and some cooking ingredients for the house." In that moment, you have done two amazing things: first, you understood (comprehended) the entire detailed list and what its purpose was, and second, you then explained it briefly and clearly (summarized) in your own words, hitting the main points without all the tiny details! That's exactly what comprehension and summary are all about in English!
What is Comprehension & Summary?

In the world of reading and understanding, "comprehension" is your superpower to truly grasp and make sense of what you read. The word "comprehend" actually comes from a Latin word meaning "to seize" or "to grasp." So, when you comprehend a passage, you're not just looking at the words on the page; you're actively grabbing onto their meaning, understanding the message the writer is trying to pass, and connecting all the ideas together. It's like being a detective, gathering all the clues (words, sentences, paragraphs) to solve the mystery of the text's meaning. Without proper comprehension, reading can feel like looking at a coded message you can't decipher.

On the flip side, "summary" is what you do *after* you have fully comprehended something. A summary is a condensed, shorter version of a longer text, where you present only the most important points or main ideas in your own words. Think of it as telling a friend about a two-hour Nollywood movie in just two minutes. You wouldn't describe every single scene or dialogue; instead, you'd focus on the key characters, the main storyline, and the overall outcome. A good summary retains the core message of the original text but strips away all the non-essential details, making it quicker and easier for someone to understand the gist without reading the whole thing.

Detailed Explanation

What is Comprehension and Why is it Essential?

Comprehension is the foundation of effective reading. It's not just about pronouncing words correctly; it's about making meaning from them. When you read, your brain is actively working to process information, identify main ideas, understand relationships between sentences, and even infer things that aren't explicitly stated. Strong comprehension skills allow you to follow instructions, learn new subjects, enjoy stories, and succeed in exams like WAEC and JAMB. If you don't comprehend, you're simply passing your eyes over words without absorbing any knowledge.

For your exams, comprehension means you need to be able to:

  • Identify the Main Idea: What is the passage primarily about? What is the central message the author wants to convey? This is often found in topic sentences or repeated themes.
  • Understand Specific Details: Who, what, when, where, why, and how? These are the facts, figures, and specific pieces of information mentioned in the text.
  • Determine Vocabulary in Context: Understand the meaning of unfamiliar words based on how they are used in the sentences around them, even if you don't know the dictionary definition.
  • Make Inferences: This is like reading between the lines. The author might not tell you everything directly, but they give you clues to figure things out. For example, if a character is shivering and hugging themselves, you can infer they are cold.
  • Identify Author's Purpose or Tone: Is the author trying to inform, persuade, entertain, or describe? What is their attitude towards the subject (e.g., serious, humorous, critical)?

How to Master Comprehension? (The OAE Step-by-Step Guide)

Don't worry if comprehension feels tricky sometimes. With practice and a good strategy, you can become a comprehension champion! Here's how:

Step 1: Read the Passage Actively (Don't just stare!)

First, quickly skim through the passage to get a general idea of what it's about. Then, read it slowly and carefully, sentence by sentence, paragraph by paragraph. Don't be afraid to read it a second time if needed. Pay attention to punctuation – commas, periods, and question marks are like traffic signs guiding your understanding.

Why? To get a first impression and then a deeper understanding. Active reading helps your brain process information better than passive reading.
Step 2: Understand the Main Idea of Each Paragraph and the Whole Passage.

As you read, ask yourself: "What is this paragraph mostly talking about?" Try to identify the topic sentence, which often states the main idea. Then, combine the main ideas of all paragraphs to find the overall main idea of the entire passage. It's like figuring out the major theme of a party.

Why? The main idea is the core message. Without it, you miss the author's point.
Step 3: Identify Supporting Details.

These are the facts, examples, explanations, and descriptions that back up the main idea. Think of them as the pillars holding up the roof (main idea) of a house. Underline or make a mental note of them as you read.

Why? Details provide evidence and clarify the main idea. Questions often test your understanding of these specifics.
Step 4: Understand Vocabulary in Context.

If you encounter an unfamiliar word, don't panic! Look at the words and sentences around it. Does the context give you a clue about its meaning? For example, if a passage says "The man was so *famished* that he devoured his food," you can infer "famished" means extremely hungry.

Why? Many exam questions test vocabulary. Context clues are your best friend when a dictionary isn't available.
Step 5: Make Inferences and Draw Conclusions.

This means using the clues provided in the text, along with your own knowledge, to figure out things that aren't directly stated. If a character says, "I wish I had packed a warmer jacket," you can infer they are cold, even if the passage doesn't explicitly say "It was cold."

Why? Inference questions are common in exams and show a deeper level of understanding.
Step 6: Read the Questions Carefully and Answer Accurately.

Before you answer, read each question *at least twice*. Understand what is being asked. Go back to the passage and find the specific information that answers the question. Don't guess or rely on memory alone. Your answer should be based ONLY on the information in the passage.

Why? Misreading a question is a common mistake that leads to losing marks. Accuracy means sticking strictly to the text.

What is Summary and Why is it Important?

A summary is a concise, objective, and accurate representation of the main points of a text, written in your own words. It's like distilling a large pot of soup down to its concentrated essence – all the flavour is there, but without the bulk. Summarizing is a powerful skill because it forces you to truly comprehend the original material before you can condense it.

Why is summary so important? Oh, my dear students, it's a life skill! Beyond exams, summarizing helps you:

  • Improve Understanding: When you summarize, you process information deeply, leading to better comprehension.
  • Remember Information: Condensing information makes it easier to recall key facts and ideas.
  • Communicate Effectively: You can quickly share the core message of a long report, article, or conversation.
  • Study Efficiently: Creating summaries of your textbooks and notes helps you prepare for exams without having to re-read everything.
  • Develop Critical Thinking: It teaches you to distinguish between essential and non-essential information.

How to Write an Excellent Summary? (The OAE Summary Blueprint)

Writing a good summary is an art, but it's an art you can master with these steps:

Step 1: Comprehend the Original Text Thoroughly.

This is the FIRST and most CRITICAL step. You cannot summarize what you do not understand. Apply all the comprehension steps discussed above. Read the passage multiple times until you are confident you know its main idea and all its important details.

Why? A summary is a reflection of your understanding. If your comprehension is faulty, your summary will be too.
Step 2: Identify the Main Idea of the Entire Passage.

What is the single most important message the author is trying to convey? This should be the central point of your summary. Think of it as the "headline" for the whole passage.

Why? Every good summary revolves around the central theme.
Step 3: Pick Out the Most Important Supporting Details.

Once you have the main idea, identify the 2-3 strongest arguments, examples, or facts that directly support that main idea. These are the details you cannot remove without changing the core message. Ignore minor examples, anecdotes, or repetitive information.

Why? These details provide context and evidence for the main idea, making the summary complete but still concise.
Step 4: Draft Your Summary in Your Own Words.

Now, write down the main idea and the selected key supporting details in your own language. DO NOT copy sentences directly from the original passage. Use synonyms and rephrase ideas. Connect your sentences smoothly, making sure the summary flows logically.

Why? Using your own words shows true understanding and avoids plagiarism. It also demonstrates your command of language.
Step 5: Check for Conciseness, Accuracy, and Completeness.

Is your summary significantly shorter than the original? Does it accurately reflect the author's message without adding your own opinions? Have you included all the crucial points without missing anything vital? Ensure it's objective.

Why? These are the hallmarks of a good summary. A summary is meant to be brief, correct, and cover the essentials.
Step 6: Review for Grammar, Spelling, and Word Count (if applicable).

Read your summary aloud to catch awkward phrasing. Check for any errors. If there's a word count limit (e.g., "in not more than 60 words"), count your words carefully and trim down if necessary.

Why? A well-written summary needs to be grammatically correct and adhere to any given instructions to earn full marks.

Key Differences Between Comprehension and Summary Questions in Exams

While comprehension is the skill, both comprehension and summary appear as distinct question types in exams like WAEC and JAMB. It's vital to know the difference:

  • Comprehension Questions: These typically ask specific questions about the passage. They might require you to locate a specific detail, explain a concept from the text, infer meaning, or identify the author's tone. Your answers should be based directly on the text, though sometimes rephrased for clarity. You don't usually need to condense the entire passage, just answer the specific query.
  • Summary Questions: These questions explicitly ask you to provide a summary of the entire passage or a specific part of it. They often come with a word count limit (e.g., "Summarize the passage in not more than 50 words"). Here, the goal is to condense the main ideas and crucial details into a much shorter form, always in your own words, and without introducing new information or personal opinions.

Worked Examples & Applications

Example 1 (Comprehension & Summary)

Passage: The harmattan season in Nigeria is characterized by dry, dusty north-easterly trade winds, blowing from the Sahara Desert over West Africa. It typically occurs between November and March. During this period, humidity is very low, visibility can be reduced due to dust haze, and temperatures often drop significantly, especially at night. People frequently experience dry skin, chapped lips, and respiratory issues. Farmers often face challenges as the dry winds can damage crops, leading to lower yields. However, the harmattan can also bring some benefits, such as cooler weather which offers relief from the usual tropical heat, and a decrease in mosquito populations due to the dry conditions. It also makes laundry dry very quickly!

Comprehension Question: According to the passage, what are two disadvantages of the harmattan season for people and two for farmers?

Step 1: Locate relevant information.

Scan the passage for keywords like "disadvantages," "people," and "farmers" related to harmattan.

Why? To find the specific sentences that contain the answer to the question.
Step 2: Extract and phrase the answer.

For people: "People frequently experience dry skin, chapped lips, and respiratory issues." We can pick two from these. For farmers: "Farmers often face challenges as the dry winds can damage crops, leading to lower yields."

Answer: Two disadvantages for people are dry skin and chapped lips. For farmers, two disadvantages are damaged crops and lower yields.

Why? Direct answers from the text, using the provided information, not opinions.

Summary Question: Summarize the provided passage in not more than 30 words, focusing on its definition and key characteristics.

Step 1: Understand the passage and identify the main idea.

The passage defines harmattan and describes its features, challenges, and some benefits.

Why? To grasp the core message before condensing.
Step 2: Identify key characteristics and definition to include.

Definition: dry, dusty north-easterly winds. Characteristics: low humidity, reduced visibility, temperature drops, dry skin, crop damage, cooler weather, fewer mosquitoes.

Why? To select only the essential information relevant to the summary question's focus.
Step 3: Draft the summary in your own words, keeping word count in mind.

The harmattan is a Nigerian dry, dusty wind from the Sahara, occurring November-March. It brings low humidity, cold nights, dry skin, and crop damage, though it also offers cooler temperatures and fewer mosquitoes.

Why? To ensure conciseness, originality, and adherence to the word limit while covering the main points.
Step 4: Review and refine.

Count words: 38 words. This is slightly over 30 words. Let's trim it down. "The harmattan is a Nigerian dry, dusty wind (November-March). It causes low humidity, dry skin, and crop damage, but also brings cooler weather and fewer mosquitoes." (29 words)

Answer: The harmattan is a Nigerian dry, dusty wind (November-March). It causes low humidity, dry skin, and crop damage, but also brings cooler weather and fewer mosquitoes.

Why? To ensure the summary meets all requirements, including the strict word count.
Watch Out! (Exam Trap)

Many students lose marks in comprehension and summary questions due to common errors. For comprehension, a big trap is not reading the question properly. You might see a keyword and jump to an answer without fully understanding what the question is asking. For example, a question might ask "What is the *primary* reason for X?" and you give a secondary reason. Always re-read the question after reading the passage to ensure you are directly addressing what is asked.

For summary questions, the biggest trap is copying sentences directly from the passage. Examiners are looking for your ability to rephrase and condense information in your own words. If you copy, you will definitely lose marks. Another common mistake is making your summary too long or too short, especially when a word limit is provided. Stick to the word count, focus only on the main ideas and crucial supporting details, and leave out your personal opinions or new information not present in the original text. Always remember to use your own language – think of it as "decoding" the author's message and then "re-encoding" it into your own, shorter message.

Conclusion & Summary

Comprehension and summary are two sides of the same coin when it comes to understanding and interacting with written information. Comprehension is the foundational skill of truly understanding what you read, digging into the main ideas, details, vocabulary, and hidden meanings. Summary, built upon strong comprehension, is the art of distilling that understanding into a concise, accurate, and objective shorter version using your own words. Mastering these skills will not only help you ace your English exams but also empower you in every aspect of your academic and daily life, making you a more effective learner and communicator. Keep practicing, keep reading actively, and you will surely excel!

  • Comprehension: The ability to understand what you read by grasping main ideas, details, vocabulary, and making inferences.
  • Summary: A shorter, condensed version of a text, presenting only the main ideas and crucial details in your own words.
  • Steps for Comprehension: Active reading, identifying main ideas/details, understanding context, inferring, and answering precisely.
  • Steps for Summary: Thorough comprehension, identifying main idea/key details, drafting in own words, checking conciseness/accuracy, and reviewing grammar/word count.
  • Avoid Exam Traps: Don't misread questions (comprehension); don't copy text or ignore word limits (summary).
