Concord (Subject-Verb Agreement)

Real-World Example: Imagine you're at a family gathering, and your mother asks, "Who wants jollof rice?" If only *one person* raises their hand, you would say, "He *wants* jollof rice." You wouldn't say, "He *want* jollof rice," because that sounds off, right? But if *many people* raise their hands, you'd say, "They *want* jollof rice." You wouldn't say, "They *wants* jollof rice." See how the word "want" changes to "wants" depending on whether it's one person or many? This simple idea of making your 'doing word' (verb) match your 'who/what' (subject) is exactly what Concord, or Subject-Verb Agreement, is all about! Just like in a marching band, every musician must be in sync; the subject and the verb in a sentence must also be in perfect harmony. If they don't agree, the sentence sounds wrong and confusing, just like a band playing out of tune.
What is Concord (Subject-Verb Agreement)?

My dear student, Concord, in simple English, is the fancy name for 'agreement'. Specifically, in grammar, when we talk about 'Concord' or 'Subject-Verb Agreement', we are simply referring to the rule that states: the verb in a sentence must agree with its subject in number. This means if your subject is singular (just one person, place, thing, or idea), your verb must also be singular. And if your subject is plural (more than one person, place, thing, or idea), your verb must also be plural. It’s like ensuring that when you're preparing a meal, you use the right amount of salt for the quantity of food; too little or too much spoils the taste!

Why is this important, you ask? Well, imagine trying to have a serious conversation, and the person you're talking to keeps saying things like "The boys is playing" or "My friend walk to school." It sounds awkward, doesn't it? That's because their subjects and verbs are not agreeing. Correct subject-verb agreement helps your sentences make sense, sound natural, and communicate your ideas clearly and effectively. It's a foundational pillar of good English, essential for passing your WAEC and JAMB exams, and for speaking and writing fluently in everyday life. Without it, your communication can become confusing, making it difficult for others to understand your message, just like trying to follow directions given in a mixed-up language.

Detailed Explanation

1. The Golden Rule: Singular Subject, Singular Verb; Plural Subject, Plural Verb

This is the most fundamental rule, the bedrock of Concord. If your subject refers to one thing, your verb will often end in '-s' in the simple present tense (e.g., *walks, sings, eats*). If your subject refers to more than one thing, your verb will usually *not* end in '-s' (e.g., *walk, sing, eat*). This is the opposite of how we form plurals for nouns (e.g., *boy* becomes *boys*, adding '-s' for plural).

Let’s look at examples:

  • Singular Subject: The girl sings beautifully. (One girl, so the verb 'sings' ends with -s).
  • Plural Subject: The girls sing beautifully. (More than one girl, so the verb 'sing' does not end with -s).
  • Singular Subject: My brother lives in Lagos.
  • Plural Subject: My brothers live in Lagos.
Remember that 'be' verbs (am, is, are, was, were) also follow this rule: *I am, he is, they are, she was, we were.*

2. Compound Subjects Joined by 'And'

When two or more subjects are joined by the word 'and', they usually form a plural subject and therefore take a plural verb. Think of it like bringing two friends together; now you have a group, which is plural!

  • My father and my uncle are going to the market. (Father + Uncle = two people, so 'are' is used).
  • A pen and a notebook were on the table.
  • Grace and James enjoy playing ludo.

However, there's a tiny exception! If the two subjects joined by 'and' refer to a single person, thing, or idea, they take a singular verb. This often happens with common phrases or titles that represent one entity.

  • Bread and butter is a good breakfast. (Considered as one meal).
  • The popular musician and producer has released a new album. (Refers to one person who is both a musician and a producer).
  • Beans and dodo is my favorite meal.

3. Compound Subjects Joined by 'Or' or 'Nor'

This rule is a bit different. When two or more subjects are joined by 'or' or 'nor', the verb agrees with the subject that is *closest* to it. Imagine there's a competition, and only the subject right next to the verb gets to decide its fate!

  • Neither the students nor the teacher is ready for the exam. (The closest subject to 'is' is 'teacher', which is singular).
  • Neither the teacher nor the students are ready for the exam. (The closest subject to 'are' is 'students', which is plural).
  • Either my mother or my sisters are coming to visit.
  • Either my sisters or my mother is coming to visit.

4. Indefinite Pronouns

Indefinite pronouns refer to non-specific people or things. They can be tricky because some *sound* plural but are always singular, while others are always plural, and some can be either. Let’s break them down:

a. Always Singular Indefinite Pronouns

These pronouns always take a singular verb, even if they seem to refer to multiple people or things. Think of them as individual units:

Each, every, either, neither, anyone, anybody, anything, someone, somebody, something, no one, nobody, nothing, everyone, everybody, everything.

  • Everyone wants a share of the suya.
  • Each of the students has submitted their assignment.
  • Neither of the options is correct.
  • Nobody knows the answer.

b. Always Plural Indefinite Pronouns

These pronouns always take a plural verb:

Both, few, many, several.

  • Both of them are very intelligent.
  • Many were called, but few were chosen.
  • Several students have passed the test.

c. Indefinite Pronouns That Can Be Singular or Plural

These pronouns take a singular or plural verb depending on the noun they refer to (the object of the prepositional phrase that follows them):

All, any, most, none, some.

  • Some of the water is spilled. (Water is uncountable, so singular).
  • Some of the students are absent. (Students are countable and plural, so plural).
  • All of the money was stolen. (Money is uncountable, so singular).
  • All of the books were returned. (Books are countable and plural, so plural).

5. Collective Nouns

Collective nouns are words that represent a group of people, animals, or things as a single unit (e.g., *family, team, committee, audience, class, government, jury, flock*). They can be either singular or plural depending on how they are acting.

  • If the group acts as a single, unified unit, the verb is singular.
    • The team is celebrating its victory. (The team is acting as one unit).
    • The committee has reached a decision.
  • If the members of the group are acting individually, or are seen as separate entities within the group, the verb is plural.
    • The team are arguing among themselves. (Individual members are arguing).
    • The family are discussing their different holiday plans.

6. Nouns Ending in -s but Singular in Meaning

Some nouns end in '-s' but are actually singular in meaning and therefore take a singular verb. Be careful not to let the '-s' trick you!

Mathematics, physics, economics, civics, news, measles, mumps, athletics, statistics (as a subject).

  • Mathematics is my favorite subject.
  • The news is broadcast every evening.
  • Measles is a contagious disease.

7. Nouns Always Plural

Some nouns are always plural and always take a plural verb, even if they refer to a single item that has two parts.

Scissors, trousers, pants, spectacles, glasses, shorts, pliers, police, people, cattle.

  • My scissors are blunt.
  • His new trousers were bought yesterday.
  • The police have arrived at the scene.
  • The cattle are grazing in the field.

However, if you use phrases like "a pair of...", then the word "pair" becomes the subject, which is singular:

  • A pair of scissors is needed for the craft project.

8. Phrases Between Subject and Verb

Often, there can be a long phrase or clause between the subject and the verb. Do not let these intervening words confuse you! The verb must agree with the *actual subject* of the sentence, not with any noun or pronoun in the intervening phrase.

  • The box of fresh mangoes is on the table. (The subject is 'box', not 'mangoes').
  • The students, along with their teacher, are going on an excursion. (The subject is 'students', not 'teacher').
  • One of the girls plays the piano very well. (The subject is 'one', not 'girls').

9. Inverted Sentences (Beginning with "There is/are" or "Here is/are")

In sentences that begin with "there is/are" or "here is/are", the true subject follows the verb. So, you must look *after* the verb to identify the subject and ensure agreement.

  • There is a book on the shelf. (Subject: 'book' - singular).
  • There are many books on the shelf. (Subject: 'books' - plural).
  • Here comes the bride. (Subject: 'bride' - singular).
  • Here come the guests. (Subject: 'guests' - plural).

10. Relative Pronouns (Who, Which, That)

When a relative pronoun (who, which, that) is the subject of a clause, the verb in that clause must agree with the *antecedent* (the noun or pronoun the relative pronoun refers to).

  • He is one of the students who excel in science. ('Who' refers to 'students', which is plural, so 'excel' is plural).
  • She is the only student who excels in science. ('Who' refers to 'student', which is singular, so 'excels' is singular).
  • This is the car that needs repairs. ('That' refers to 'car', which is singular).
  • These are the cars that need repairs. ('That' refers to 'cars', which is plural).

11. Units of Measurement, Time, Money, and Distance

When these refer to a single quantity or a unified concept, they generally take a singular verb, even if the number looks plural.

  • Ten thousand Naira is a lot of money. (Considered a single sum).
  • Five years is a long time to wait. (Considered a single duration).
  • Fifty kilometers is a short distance for him. (Considered a single distance).
  • Two-thirds of the land is fertile. (Refers to a single portion of land).

However, if these units refer to individual items within the quantity, they can be plural:

  • Twenty-four hours in a day go by so fast. (Referring to the individual hours).

12. Titles of Books, Plays, Movies, and Organizations

Even if titles appear plural, they represent a single work or entity and therefore take a singular verb.

  • "Things Fall Apart" is a famous novel by Chinua Achebe.
  • "The Living Dead" was a terrifying movie.
  • United Nations works for peace.

Worked Examples & Applications

Example 1 (Compound Subject)

Choose the correct verb: Neither Femi nor his sisters (is / are) at home.

Step 1: Identify the subjects and the conjunction.

The subjects are 'Femi' (singular) and 'his sisters' (plural). They are joined by 'nor'.

Why? Knowing the conjunction ('or'/'nor') tells you to look at the closest subject.
Step 2: Apply the rule for 'or'/'nor'.

The rule states that when subjects are joined by 'or' or 'nor', the verb agrees with the subject *closest* to it.

Why? This specific rule handles compound subjects that don't act as a unified plural.
Step 3: Determine the number of the closest subject.

The closest subject to the verb position is 'his sisters', which is plural.

Why? The verb must match 'sisters'.
Step 4: Select the verb that agrees with the closest subject.

Since 'sisters' is plural, the correct verb is 'are'.

Answer: Neither Femi nor his sisters are at home.

Why? Plural subject ('sisters') takes a plural verb ('are').
Example 2 (Intervening Phrase)

Fill in the blank: One of the students in the class (is / are) absent today.

Step 1: Identify the main subject of the sentence.

The main subject is 'One'. The phrase 'of the students in the class' is an intervening prepositional phrase that describes 'one'.

Why? Don't let the words in between confuse you; the verb must agree with the *true* subject.
Step 2: Determine the number of the main subject.

'One' is clearly a singular subject.

Why? The number of the subject dictates the verb form.
Step 3: Select the verb that agrees with the singular subject.

Since 'one' is singular, the correct verb is 'is'.

Answer: One of the students in the class is absent today.

Why? Singular subject ('one') takes a singular verb ('is').
Example 3 (Collective Noun)

Choose the correct verb: The audience (was / were) cheering loudly as the singer performed.

Step 1: Identify the subject and determine if it's acting as a unit or individually.

The subject is 'The audience', which is a collective noun. The phrase 'cheering loudly as the singer performed' suggests the audience is acting as one unified group, all doing the same action together.

Why? Collective nouns' agreement depends on their action (unified or individual).
Step 2: Apply the collective noun rule.

If the collective noun acts as a single unit, it takes a singular verb.

Why? Unified action means singular agreement.
Step 3: Select the verb that agrees with the singular collective noun.

Since the audience is acting as one unit, the correct verb is 'was'.

Answer: The audience was cheering loudly as the singer performed.

Why? Collective noun ('audience') acting as a unit takes a singular verb ('was').
Watch Out! (Exam Trap)

A very common trap in WAEC and JAMB exams involves intervening phrases, especially those starting with "of" or "along with/as well as". Students often get confused and make the verb agree with the noun *inside* the phrase rather than the actual subject.

For example, you might see: "The quality of the locally made shoes (is / are) very good." Many students quickly see "shoes" (plural) and choose "are". But the actual subject is "quality" (singular), and "of the locally made shoes" is just telling us *whose* quality we are talking about. So the correct answer is "is".

Another tricky one is with phrases like "along with" or "as well as": "The president, as well as his ministers, (is / are) attending the summit." Here, "The president" is the main subject (singular), and "as well as his ministers" is just additional information, not part of the compound subject. Therefore, the verb should be "is". Always identify the *real* subject before choosing your verb!

Conclusion & Summary

My dear student, understanding Concord or Subject-Verb Agreement is like knowing the secret handshake of English grammar. It makes your sentences strong, clear, and correct. It might seem like a lot of rules, but with practice, you'll find that these rules become second nature, just like learning to ride a bicycle. Always remember that the key is to correctly identify the subject of your sentence, decide if it's singular or plural, and then pick the verb that matches its number. If you get confused, break the sentence down, ignore extra information, and focus on the core subject and its verb. Keep practicing, and you'll master this important skill for your exams and beyond!

  • The verb must agree in number with its subject: singular subject takes singular verb; plural subject takes plural verb.
  • Subjects joined by 'and' usually take a plural verb, unless they refer to a single entity.
  • Subjects joined by 'or' or 'nor' agree with the subject closest to the verb.
  • Indefinite pronouns like 'everyone', 'each', 'nobody' are always singular.
  • Watch out for intervening phrases between the subject and the verb; the verb must agree with the main subject.
  • Collective nouns can be singular (acting as a unit) or plural (acting individually).
  • Nouns like 'news', 'mathematics' are singular despite ending in '-s'.
  • In "there is/are" sentences, the subject comes after the verb.
