General Literary Principles

Real-World Example:

My dear student, imagine you're watching a thrilling Nollywood movie like "The Wedding Party" or listening to a powerful song by Burna Boy. What makes these creations so enjoyable, so impactful, and sometimes even makes you laugh or cry? It's not just the actors or the singer! It's because the people who created them – the scriptwriters, directors, and songwriters – understand certain 'secrets' or 'tools' that make their work shine. They know how to tell a story effectively, how to make you feel emotions, and how to pass a message. These 'secrets' or 'tools' are exactly what we call 'General Literary Principles' in literature. They are the ingredients and recipes that make good literature!

What are General Literary Principles?

General Literary Principles are simply the fundamental rules, concepts, ideas, and tools that writers use to create different types of literary works, such as poems, stories, novels, and plays. Think of them as the basic building blocks and guidelines that shape any piece of writing. Just like a carpenter needs to know about wood, nails, and different tools to build a beautiful chair, a writer needs to understand these principles to craft a compelling story or a moving poem.

Understanding these principles is crucial for you as a student of literature because they help you to properly analyze, understand, and appreciate any literary text you read. When you know these principles, you'll be able to look beyond just the words on the page and see how the writer has skillfully put things together to achieve a particular effect, convey a specific message, or evoke certain emotions in you, the reader. It helps you to 'decode' the writer's work and truly grasp its meaning and artistry.

Detailed Explanation: The Building Blocks of Literature

Now, let's dive deep into these powerful principles. We'll break them down into categories so you can understand them perfectly, step-by-step. Don't worry, we'll use simple language and lots of examples!

1. Literary Genres: The Different Types of Literature

Just like we have different types of food (soup, stew, rice), literature also comes in different categories called genres. The three main genres you'll often encounter are Prose, Poetry, and Drama.

  • Prose: This is the most common form of written language, the way we speak and write every day, without any special line breaks or rhythmic patterns like in poetry. It's written in sentences and paragraphs.
    • Examples: Novels (like "Things Fall Apart" by Chinua Achebe), short stories, essays, biographies, newspaper articles.
    • Characteristics:
      • Uses everyday language.
      • Organized into sentences and paragraphs.
      • Focuses on telling a story, providing information, or discussing an idea.
      • Has elements like plot, characters, setting, and theme.
  • Poetry: Poetry is a special way of writing that uses language creatively and musically. It often uses rhythm, rhyme, and imagery to express feelings, ideas, or tell a story in a very concise and powerful way.
    • Examples: "The Village Blacksmith" by Henry Wadsworth Longfellow, Niyi Osundare's poems.
    • Characteristics:
      • Often divided into lines and stanzas (groups of lines).
      • May have a specific rhythm (meter) and rhyme scheme.
      • Uses figurative language extensively (simile, metaphor, personification).
      • Focuses on emotions, abstract ideas, or sensory experiences.
  • Drama: Drama refers to stories written specifically to be performed on a stage by actors. It uses dialogue and stage directions to tell a story.
    • Examples: "The Lion and the Jewel" by Wole Soyinka, "Romeo and Juliet" by William Shakespeare.
    • Characteristics:
      • Written in dialogue (conversations between characters).
      • Includes stage directions (instructions for actors and stage crew).
      • Divided into acts and scenes.
      • Designed for performance, involving costumes, sets, and props.

2. Elements of Literature: The Essential Parts

These are the core components you find in most literary works, especially prose and drama.

  • Plot: This is the sequence of events in a story. It's what happens from the beginning to the end. Think of it as the storyline.
    • Structure: Usually includes an exposition (introduction), rising action (build-up), climax (turning point), falling action (wrap-up), and resolution (ending).
  • Characterization: This is how the writer creates and develops the personalities of the people (or animals/things) in a story. It tells us who they are.
    • Types of Characters: Protagonist (main character, hero), Antagonist (character opposing the protagonist), Round (complex), Flat (simple), Dynamic (changes), Static (doesn't change).
  • Setting: This refers to the time and place where the story happens. It can be specific (e.g., Lagos, 1980) or general (e.g., a small village).
    • Importance: Setting can influence the mood, atmosphere, and even the characters' actions.
  • Theme: This is the main idea, message, or underlying meaning that the writer wants to convey. It's the 'lesson' or 'insight' about life or human nature that the story explores.
    • Examples: Love, hatred, justice, corruption, sacrifice, revenge, the struggle for freedom.
  • Conflict: This is the struggle or problem that characters face in a story. It's what drives the plot forward.
    • Types: Character vs. Character, Character vs. Self (internal struggle), Character vs. Society, Character vs. Nature, Character vs. Fate/Supernatural.
  • Point of View (POV): This is the perspective from which the story is told. Who is narrating the story?
    • First Person: The narrator is a character in the story, using "I" and "me". (e.g., "I walked to the market...")
    • Third Person Omniscient: The narrator is outside the story but knows everything about all characters and events (their thoughts, feelings, etc.).
    • Third Person Limited: The narrator is outside the story but only knows the thoughts and feelings of one specific character.
  • Tone: This is the writer's attitude towards the subject matter or the audience. It's like the 'voice' or 'feeling' you get from the writing.
    • Examples: Serious, humorous, sarcastic, critical, joyful, melancholic.
  • Mood/Atmosphere: This is the feeling or emotion that the reader experiences while reading the text. It's created by the setting, imagery, and tone.
    • Examples: Suspenseful, romantic, gloomy, peaceful, frightening.

3. Literary Devices (Figures of Speech): The Writer's Special Tools

These are techniques or tools writers use to make their language more colorful, expressive, and impactful. They add depth and beauty to the writing.

  • Simile: A comparison between two unlike things using "like" or "as".
    • Example: "Her smile was as bright as the morning sun." (Comparing smile to sun using 'as').
    • Example: "He fought like a wounded lion." (Comparing his fighting to a lion using 'like').
  • Metaphor: A direct comparison between two unlike things, stating that one thing is another, without using "like" or "as".
    • Example: "The classroom was a zoo after break time." (Stating classroom is a zoo directly).
    • Example: "He is a cheetah on the football field." (Stating he is a cheetah directly).
  • Personification: Giving human qualities or actions to inanimate objects, animals, or abstract ideas.
    • Example: "The wind whispered secrets through the trees." (Wind cannot whisper like a human).
    • Example: "The sun smiled down on the children." (Sun cannot smile).
  • Hyperbole: An extreme exaggeration used for emphasis or effect.
    • Example: "I'm so hungry I could eat a whole cow!" (Exaggerating hunger).
    • Example: "I've told you a million times to be careful!" (Exaggerating the number of times).
  • Imagery: Language that appeals to the five senses (sight, sound, smell, taste, touch) to create vivid mental pictures or sensations for the reader.
    • Example: "The sizzling sound of garri frying, combined with the pungent aroma of pepper and onions, filled the kitchen." (Appeals to sound and smell).
    • Example: "The rough, cold surface of the rock felt like sandpaper under my fingertips." (Appeals to touch).
  • Symbolism: The use of an object, person, place, or idea to represent something else, often a deeper, abstract idea.
    • Example: A white dove often symbolizes peace.
    • Example: The Nigerian green and white flag symbolizes our nation's agriculture and peace/unity.
  • Alliteration: The repetition of the same beginning consonant sound in words that are close together.
    • Example: "Peter Piper picked a peck of pickled peppers."
  • Onomatopoeia: Words that imitate the sound they represent.
    • Example: "The dog went woof woof!" "The clock went tick-tock." "The snake hissed."
  • Irony: A contrast or discrepancy between what is said and what is actually meant, or between what is expected to happen and what actually happens.
    • Verbal Irony: Saying the opposite of what you mean (like sarcasm).
      Example: You step out into a heavy downpour and say, "What lovely weather!"
    • Situational Irony: When the outcome of a situation is contrary to what was expected.
      Example: A fire station burns down. (You'd expect a fire station to be safe from fire).
    • Dramatic Irony: When the audience knows something that the characters in the story do not.
      Example: In a horror movie, the audience knows the killer is hiding in the closet, but the character doesn't.
  • Foreshadowing: Hints or clues given by the writer about events that will happen later in the story. It builds suspense.
    • Example: A character repeatedly mentions a dark dream they had, which later turns out to be a prediction of a coming danger.
  • Flashback: An interruption in the story's sequence of events to show an event that happened in the past. It helps to provide background information.
    • Example: A character is struggling with a fear, and the story suddenly jumps back to an incident in their childhood that caused that fear.

Worked Examples & Applications

Let's put some of these principles into practice! We'll look at a short excerpt and identify some of the literary principles at work.

Example 1: Analyzing a Poem Excerpt

Read the following lines from a poem and identify two literary devices used:

"The moon was a giant golden ball in the dark sky,
Watching over the silent village below.
The hungry wind howled its mournful cry,
As shadows danced in the flickering glow."

Step 1: Read carefully and look for comparisons or human actions.

First, read the lines slowly, paying attention to any unusual descriptions or strong verbs.

Why? Literary devices often make language stand out from everyday speech.
Step 2: Identify potential devices.

Look at the first line: "The moon was a giant golden ball..." Here, the moon is directly compared to a golden ball. This doesn't use 'like' or 'as'. This is a Metaphor.

Next, look at "Watching over the silent village below" and "The hungry wind howled its mournful cry, As shadows danced in the flickering glow."

Can the moon 'watch'? Can the wind be 'hungry' or 'howl' like an animal (or person)? Can shadows 'dance'? No, these are human or animal actions given to inanimate things. This is Personification.

Why? Understanding definitions of devices helps you match them to the text.
Step 3: State your answer clearly.

Two literary devices used are: Metaphor ("The moon was a giant golden ball") and Personification ("The hungry wind howled," "shadows danced," "moon...Watching").

Why? Always back up your answer with the specific line or phrase from the text.
Watch Out! (Exam Trap)

My dear students, one of the biggest traps in JAMB and WAEC exams is confusing Simile and Metaphor. Many students see any comparison and automatically call it a simile. Remember, the key difference is the presence of "like" or "as" for simile. If they are missing, it's a metaphor!

Wrong example: "The boy is a lion on the field" is a simile. (Incorrect! It's a metaphor because 'is' is used, not 'like' or 'as').

Correct thinking:

  • Simile: "He fought like a lion." (Uses 'like')
  • Metaphor: "He is a lion in battle." (Direct statement, no 'like' or 'as')

Also, when asked for the theme of a story, don't just summarize the plot! The plot is what happens; the theme is the deeper message or lesson about life. For example, the plot might be about a boy who goes to school, but the theme could be 'the importance of education' or 'overcoming adversity'. Always think beyond the surface level.

Conclusion & Summary

Bravo! We've covered a lot of ground today, and I hope you now see that understanding General Literary Principles is like having a special key to unlock the true beauty and meaning in any literary work. These principles are not just abstract ideas; they are the practical tools writers use to create, and the critical tools you use to understand and appreciate what you read. Keep practicing their identification, and soon you'll be a master literary analyst!

  • General Literary Principles are the basic rules, concepts, and tools writers use.
  • The three main Literary Genres are Prose, Poetry, and Drama, each with unique characteristics.
  • Key Elements of Literature include Plot, Character, Setting, Theme, Conflict, Point of View, Tone, and Mood.
  • Literary Devices (Figures of Speech) like Simile, Metaphor, Personification, Hyperbole, Imagery, Symbolism, Alliteration, Onomatopoeia, Irony, Foreshadowing, and Flashback add depth and beauty to writing.
  • Always differentiate between Simile and Metaphor by looking for "like" or "as".
  • A Theme is the main message, not just the plot summary.
