Prose: The Language of Our Everyday Stories

Real-World Example:

My dear student, imagine you just got back from school and your mum asks you, "My pickin, how was school today?" And you start telling her, "Mummy, today was so long! First, Mrs. Ade talked about multiplication, then we had break time where I shared my puff-puff with Tunde, and after that, we read a story about a tortoise and a hare." You've just told her a story, flowing naturally, without trying to make it rhyme or sound like a song. Or think about reading a newspaper article about the latest football match, or a text message from your friend. All of these – your everyday conversations, stories, news, and even your textbooks – are examples of prose! It's the language we use most of the time to communicate, plain and simple.

What is Prose?

Alright, let's dive in! In Literature, when we talk about 'Prose', we are simply referring to language that is written or spoken in its ordinary, natural form. It's the language that flows freely, just like how you talk, write an essay, or tell a story to your friend. Unlike poetry, which often has a specific rhythm, rhyme scheme, and is broken into distinct lines, prose doesn't follow any strict metrical (rhythmic) structure or set line breaks. It uses complete sentences, grouped into paragraphs, and follows regular grammatical rules. Think of it as the 'everyday talk' of literature – straightforward and direct.

So, why do we call it "The Language of Our Everyday Stories"? Because most of the stories we read, the news we consume, the letters we write, and even the history books we study are all written in prose. It’s the dominant form of written and spoken language used for conveying information, telling tales, expressing ideas, and generally communicating in a clear and understandable way. From novels by great Nigerian authors like Chinua Achebe and Wole Soyinka, to the articles you read online, prose is everywhere, making sense of our world through logical, sequential sentences and paragraphs.

Detailed Explanation

Now that we know what prose is, let's explore it deeper. Understanding prose is like learning the different ingredients that make up a delicious pot of jollof rice – each part plays a crucial role in the final taste!

Characteristics of Prose

Prose has certain qualities that make it stand out from other forms of writing, especially poetry. Let's look at them:

  • No Specific Rhythmic Pattern: Unlike poetry that might have a 'beat' or 'meter' (like "Twinkle, twinkle, little star"), prose doesn't try to make its words sound musical. It flows naturally, just like regular speech. You won't find it counting syllables or rhyming at the end of every line.
  • Grammar and Natural Speech Flow: Prose follows the standard rules of grammar. It uses full sentences, commas, periods, and all the punctuation marks to make ideas clear. It's designed to sound like someone is talking or explaining something to you directly.
  • Paragraph Structure: Ideas in prose are usually grouped into paragraphs. Each paragraph typically focuses on a single main idea or a related set of ideas, providing structure and making it easy for the reader to follow the flow of thought. Imagine building a house; paragraphs are like the rooms, each serving a distinct purpose but connected to the whole house.
  • Variety in Sentence Structure: A good prose writer uses a mix of short, punchy sentences and longer, more complex ones. This variety keeps the reader engaged and helps to convey different nuances of meaning. If every sentence was short, it would sound choppy; if every sentence was long, it would be tiring to read.
  • Focus on Content and Meaning: The primary goal of prose is to clearly convey information, tell a story, or express an argument. While style and beautiful language are important, they serve the main purpose of communication, not just to create an aesthetic (beautiful) pattern of words.

Types of Prose

Prose isn't just one big thing; it comes in different flavours, depending on its purpose. We can broadly divide it into two main categories:

  • Fiction Prose: This is the type of prose that tells imagined stories. The characters, events, and settings are created from the author's imagination.
    • Novels: Long fictional narratives, like Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie's "Half of a Yellow Sun" or Chinua Achebe's "Things Fall Apart."
    • Short Stories: Shorter fictional narratives, like the ones you find in storybooks or collections.
    • Novellas: Longer than a short story but shorter than a novel.
    The purpose of fiction prose is often to entertain, explore human experiences, or comment on society through made-up scenarios.
  • Non-Fiction Prose: This type of prose deals with facts, real events, and real people. It aims to inform, explain, or persuade.
    • Essays: Short pieces of writing that express the author's argument or opinion on a particular subject.
    • Biographies: Stories of a person's life written by someone else.
    • Autobiographies/Memoirs: Stories of a person's life written by that person.
    • News Articles: Reports of current events you read in newspapers or online.
    • Textbooks: Books used for studying, filled with factual information.
    • Speeches: Formal addresses delivered to an audience.
    Non-fiction prose relies on accuracy and often uses evidence or research to support its claims.
  • Prose Poetry: This is a slightly tricky one! It's a form of writing that looks like prose (it's written in paragraphs, not lines) but has many elements of poetry, such as intense imagery, figurative language (metaphors, similes), strong emotional content, and musicality. It blurs the lines between prose and poetry, giving you the natural flow of prose with the artistic density of poetry.

Elements of Prose (What to Look For When You Read)

When you're reading a story or an article, especially for your exams, you'll need to know how to identify these key elements. They are like the building blocks that make the prose effective and meaningful:

  • Plot: This is the sequence of events in a story. What happens first, next, and last? It includes the introduction, rising action, climax, falling action, and resolution.
  • Characters: These are the people, animals, or even imaginary beings that carry out the actions in a story. You'll analyze their traits, motivations, and how they develop.
  • Setting: This refers to the time and place where the story happens. Is it in a village in the 1960s, or a futuristic city in 2050? The setting often influences the characters and plot.
  • Theme: The central message, idea, or lesson that the author wants to convey. It's the deeper meaning that stays with you long after you've finished reading. For example, the theme of "Things Fall Apart" could be the clash of cultures or the destructive nature of change.
  • Point of View (POV): This tells us who is narrating or telling the story.
    • First Person: The narrator is a character in the story, using "I" and "me."
    • Third Person: The narrator is outside the story, referring to characters as "he," "she," "they." This can be **omniscient** (all-knowing) or **limited** (knowing only one character's thoughts).
  • Style: This is the author's unique way of writing. It includes their choice of words (diction), sentence structure (syntax), use of imagery, and overall tone. Some authors have a simple, direct style, while others use very elaborate and descriptive language.
  • Tone: This refers to the author's attitude towards the subject matter or audience. Is it serious, humorous, sarcastic, critical, or sympathetic?

Key Features of Strong Prose Analysis

Effective Prose Analysis = Identifying (Plot + Characters + Setting + Theme + POV + Style + Tone) + Explaining THEIR IMPACT
  • Plot: The sequence of events that drive the narrative.
  • Characters: The individuals whose actions and personalities unfold the story.
  • Setting: The specific time and place crucial to the narrative's context.
  • Theme: The underlying message or central idea conveyed by the author.
  • POV (Point of View): The perspective from which the story is told.
  • Style: The author's unique way of using language (diction, syntax, imagery).
  • Tone: The author's attitude or feeling towards the subject matter.
  • Their Impact: How these elements contribute to the overall meaning and effect of the text.

Worked Examples & Applications

Example 1: Identifying Prose Characteristics

Read the following excerpt and explain why it is an example of prose, identifying at least two key elements of prose present in it.

"The old woman sat by the window, watching the children play football in the dusty compound. A faint smile touched her lips as she remembered her own youth, full of laughter and endless games under the scorching sun. The smell of boiling yam wafted from the kitchen, a comforting scent that had filled her home for decades."

Step 1: Analyze the structure and flow of the text.

The text is written in full sentences, grouped into a paragraph. It flows naturally like everyday speech, without any strict rhyme or rhythm. There are no deliberate line breaks like in poetry; the lines continue until a period marks the end of a thought.

Why? This confirms it follows the basic structural definition of prose – natural, grammatical sentences in paragraphs.
Step 2: Identify specific elements within the text.

One clear element is **Character**: "The old woman" is the central character here, and we get a glimpse into her thoughts and feelings. Another element is **Setting**: We can infer the setting as a "dusty compound" where children play football, and there's a kitchen where "boiling yam" is cooking, suggesting a typical African home environment. Also, there's a subtle hint of **Theme**: perhaps nostalgia, the passage of time, or the comfort of home.

Why? These elements (character, setting, theme) are fundamental components of prose narratives, helping to build the story and convey meaning. They are not primarily concerned with sound or rhythm, but with content and context.
Step 3: Conclude why it's prose based on the analysis.

This excerpt is prose because it tells a story in a straightforward, narrative manner using complete sentences and paragraphs. It focuses on relaying events and the character's internal experience rather than adhering to poetic devices like rhyme or meter. The presence of a clear character and setting further solidifies its identity as prose.

Why? This step synthesizes the observations to definitively answer the question, linking structural and elemental characteristics to the definition of prose.
Watch Out! (Exam Trap)

My brilliant students, one common mistake in exams like WAEC or JAMB is confusing prose with poetry, especially when you encounter "prose poetry." Remember, the defining feature of prose is its natural, grammatical flow, presented in sentences and paragraphs, without a strict metrical pattern or line breaks designed for rhythm and rhyme. Even if a piece of prose uses beautiful imagery and figurative language (which it often does!), if it's written in continuous sentences and paragraphs like a story or an essay, it's prose. Don't be fooled by flowery language into thinking it's poetry if it lacks the structural elements of poetry (stanzas, specific line breaks, meter, or rhyme scheme). Always look at the *form* first: Is it written like a paragraph or like a verse with lines? That's your first clue!

Conclusion & Summary

So there you have it! Prose is the bedrock of our everyday communication and storytelling. It's the most common form of written and spoken language, found everywhere from your favourite novel to the morning newspaper. By understanding its natural, flowing structure and its key elements like plot, characters, setting, and theme, you're not just reading words; you're unlocking the powerful stories, ideas, and information that shape our world. Keep practicing, keep reading, and soon you'll be a master at analyzing any prose piece that comes your way!

  • Prose is ordinary, natural language, written in sentences and paragraphs.
  • It lacks a strict rhythmic pattern or specific line breaks found in poetry.
  • It follows standard grammatical rules and aims for clear communication.
  • Main types include Fiction Prose (novels, short stories) and Non-Fiction Prose (essays, biographies, news articles).
  • Key elements for analysis are Plot, Characters, Setting, Theme, Point of View, Style, and Tone.
